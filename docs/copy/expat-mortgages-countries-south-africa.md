# UK Mortgages for British Expats in South Africa

A clear guide to UK mortgages when you live and work in South Africa. What you can borrow, how lenders treat South African rand income, what deposit you will need, and how a specialist broker finds the right lender for your situation.

*9 min read · 8 sections*

---

## Who this page is for

If you are a British national living in South Africa and you want a mortgage on UK property, this page is for you.

That includes buying a UK home while you are still based in Johannesburg, Cape Town, Durban, or elsewhere, building a UK buy-to-let portfolio remotely, remortgaging an existing UK property as your fix ends, and planning a return to the UK in the next year or two.

The British expat community in South Africa includes long-term residents, dual citizens, and professionals on work assignments. Many are also planning a return to the UK and remortgaging or buying ahead of that move.

## The currency picture

You earn in South African rand. UK lenders convert ZAR income to sterling for affordability. Most apply a haircut of around 20 percent, which means a 1,500,000 ZAR package is treated as roughly 1,200,000 ZAR before the rate kicks in.

A smaller group of specialist lenders, accessed through brokers with the right relationships, do not apply a haircut at all. This is the most important variable on what you can borrow from South Africa.

The rand has been one of the more volatile major currencies against sterling, which means lenders treat ZAR income with more caution than USD, EUR, or AED. Some lenders apply heavier haircuts than the standard 20 percent for ZAR specifically. Lender choice matters more here than in most other countries.

## Common situations

British expats applying from South Africa usually fall into one of these:

- **Mining, energy, and resources** at Anglo American, BHP, and the broader sector. Senior professional roles, often well documented.
- **Banking and financial services** in Johannesburg and Cape Town at Standard Bank, FirstRand, and the international firms.
- **Healthcare** at private hospital groups and pharma. Doctors and specialists are particularly common in this profile.
- **Tech and product roles** in Cape Town at Naspers/Prosus and the broader Cape Town tech cluster.
- **Wine, agriculture, and food** in the Western Cape.
- **Tourism and hospitality** management at the lodge groups, hotel chains, and broader sector.

## What lenders want to see

The standard pack from a South Africa-based applicant:

- Three months of South African bank statements showing salary credits
- Three months of payslips
- The most recent two years of SARS IT3(a) employment income tax certificates and ITA34 assessments
- Employment contract or letter from your employer
- Evidence of South African residency (ID book or smart card, plus visa documentation if not a citizen)
- Proof of UK address history and any UK property already owned
- Two years of audited accounts if you are self-employed or run a business

South Africa has reasonable documentation but the system is paper-based in places. Lenders sometimes ask for additional verification beyond what is needed in other countries.

## How lenders view South Africa-based applicants

UK expat lenders that are active in South Africa are comfortable with the country, but the lender pool is smaller than for most other expat markets. Currency volatility is the main reason. Some lenders apply heavier haircuts on ZAR income, others avoid the rand entirely.

The right lender for a South Africa-based applicant is one with current experience of ZAR income, current ZAR-GBP exchange rates priced into the haircut sensibly, and a willingness to underwrite South African employer documentation efficiently. The wrong lender will either decline or apply a haircut that makes the loan size uneconomic.

Senior professionals at large international employers are generally well received. Smaller employers and self-employed applicants face more scrutiny.

## Common pitfalls

A few things trip up South Africa-based applications more than they should:

- **Excessive haircut.** Some lenders apply 30 to 40 percent to ZAR income rather than the standard 20 percent. Worth knowing in advance.
- **Currency timing on remortgage.** ZAR-GBP can move 10 to 15 percent in a few months. Planning ahead matters more than in most countries.
- **Source of deposit on Reserve Bank approvals.** Funds taken out of South Africa under the Reserve Bank annual allowance need clear documentation.
- **Self-employment through trusts.** South African business structures often involve family trusts. Lenders sometimes find these complex to underwrite.
- **AML on cross-border transfers.** Money moving from ZAR to GBP, often through multiple steps, needs a clean paper trail.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I borrow as much from South Africa as I could in the UK?**
Often less, because of the haircut and ZAR volatility. With a no-haircut lender, sometimes more.

**Do I need UK income to apply?**
No. South African income alone is accepted.

**Will my bonus count?**
Often, but treatment varies between lenders.

**How big a deposit do I need?**
25 percent is the working assumption for residential, 25 to 30 percent for buy-to-let.

**Does my South African status matter?**
Most lenders accept SA citizens and permanent residents. Work visa holders may face more scrutiny.

**Can I use a South African bank statement?**
Yes. Accepted directly.

**Is currency volatility an issue?**
It can be. Some lenders apply heavier haircuts on ZAR. The right lender prices the haircut sensibly.

**What about Reserve Bank limits on transferring funds?**
Reserve Bank approvals are routine and well understood by UK lenders. Documentation needs to be clear.

**Can I remortgage from South Africa?**
Yes. Common as a fix end approaches.

**How long does it take?**
Six to twelve weeks from application to offer. Slightly longer than for most expat markets.

**Do I need to come back to the UK to sign?**
No. South African Commissioner of Oaths or notary documents are accepted, as is notarisation through the British High Commission.
