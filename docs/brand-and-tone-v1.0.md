## 1. Purpose

This document defines how UK Mortgages Worldwide sounds. It governs every word published on the site, every form field label, every error message, every email auto-response. It is the reference Claude Code, future copywriters, and the founder check before publishing anything.

The single goal is conversions. Tone is a means, not an end. If a tone choice does not contribute to credibility, traffic, or conversion, it does not belong here.

## 2. Brand position in one paragraph

UK Mortgages Worldwide is the place to start when you live outside the UK and need a mortgage on UK property. The site explains what is possible, sets realistic expectations on lender appetite and criteria, and connects qualified enquiries to FCA-authorised specialist mortgage brokers who handle the application end to end. The site does not give regulated advice. It gives information of a quality that justifies the introducer relationship and earns the click.

## 3. Voice principles

Six principles. Every piece of copy can be tested against them.

**Credible.** Statements are accurate, specific, and supportable. Numbers come with context. Lender behaviour is described as we know it to be, not as we wish it were. We never overclaim. We never use weasel words ("could", "may potentially") where firmer language is honest.

**Informed.** Content reflects real depth in the niche. Expat lender criteria, currency haircuts, ICR calculations, the difference between non-resident and expat from a lender's point of view. We know this stuff, and the writing shows it. The reader should finish a page knowing more than they did, and trusting that we know more still.

**Professional.** Sentences are clean. Structure is logical. Tone is consistent across pages. We are not chatty, we are not formal, we are exactly the register of a competent specialist explaining their work to a smart client. No filler, no hedging, no jargon-as-decoration.

**Interesting.** This is the hardest one and the one most often skipped. Most mortgage content is dull, generic, written-by-committee. We earn attention by being specific. Real numbers. Worked examples. The actual reason a lender said no last week. A direct opinion where one is warranted. Boring is a competitive failure.

**Fast.** Conversion-focused. We do not pad. CTAs are explicit. Forms are short. The reader's time is treated as more valuable than ours. This applies to writing rhythm too, short sentences pull the reader through.

**Useful.** Every page leaves the reader with a next step they can actually take. Either they enquire (best), or they self-serve information that helps them, or they leave knowing what to ask the next person they speak to. Pages that are interesting but not useful do not belong here.

## 4. Voice characteristics

The principles in section 3 set direction. The patterns below are the implementation.

### Sentence structure

Vary length deliberately. Short, declarative sentences carry conviction. They are good for openings, conclusions, and CTAs. Mid-length sentences carry the bulk of the explanation. Long sentences are rare and used for genuinely complex points where breaking apart would hide the relationship between ideas.

Lead with the answer. The first sentence of every section gives the headline conclusion. The rest of the paragraph supports it. Inverted pyramid, every time.

Active voice as default. "HSBC requires three years of UK address history." Not "Three years of UK address history is required by HSBC." Passive voice is reserved for cases where the actor genuinely does not matter or is unknown.

### Sentence length distribution

Aim roughly: 15-20% under 8 words, 60% in the 10-25 word range, 15% in the 25-35 range, 5% longer. Read paragraphs aloud. If three sentences in a row sit in the 25+ word band, two of them are too long.

### Vocabulary

Use specialist terms when they are the right word. Do not euphemise "ICR" as "rental cover ratio". The reader Googling these terms knows them or wants to learn them, both audiences are served by accuracy.

Define on first use, when the term is technical and the page is not a deep specialist hub. "ICR (interest cover ratio)" first time, "ICR" thereafter.

Avoid filler vocabulary. Words to cut on sight: actually, basically, simply, literally, really, very, just, even, that, of course. They almost never carry meaning.

Numbers carry weight. Use them. "From 25% deposit" beats "lower deposits available". "Immediately" beats "quickly".

### Person and address: who is "we"?

This is a lead generation site, not a brokerage. The persona is split into two voices, and they must not be confused.

**The site speaks as "we" only when describing what the site itself does.** That means: editorial activity (we explain, we cover, we focus on), the platform mechanic (we accept enquiries, we forward them), and the introducer position (we work with FCA-authorised brokers). Examples:

- "We focus on UK mortgages for expats and non-residents."
- "We pass your enquiry to a specialist mortgage broker."
- "We do not provide regulated advice."

**Response time, advice, suitability, lender selection, and any regulated activity is attributed to "a mortgage broker" or "a specialist".** The site cannot promise these things. The broker can. Examples:

- "A mortgage broker will usually respond immediately."
- "A specialist will assess your circumstances and recommend a lender."
- "Your broker will manage the application end to end."

What this means in practice: any sentence about response time, advice, recommendations, or applications is attributed to the broker, not the site. Any sentence about content, scope, platform mechanics is attributed to the site as "we". This is not a stylistic choice, it is a compliance and accuracy requirement.

The reader is **"you"**, always. Direct address.

The brand never uses "I". Never uses third person about itself ("UK Mortgages Worldwide is...").

### Tense

Present tense as default. "Halifax considers foreign income from selected countries." Not "Halifax will consider..." or "Halifax has considered...".

Future tense is reserved for the response promise and the application process. "A mortgage broker will usually respond immediately." "Your broker will guide you through the application."

### British English

British English spellings throughout. Organise, colour, specialise, mortgaged. UK-specific phrasing where it differs ("buy-to-let" not "rental property", "freehold/leasehold" not "ownership type").

Currency formatting: £100,000 (no space, full pound sign). For non-GBP figures in body copy: "$500,000 (USD)" or "EUR 500,000" depending on what reads cleanly in context.

Dates: "29 April 2026" not "April 29 2026" and never "29/04/26". For scanability in tables, "Apr 2026" is acceptable.

### Punctuation house rules

No em-dashes ever. Replace with full stops, commas, en dashes (–), or parentheses depending on what the sentence needs.

No semicolons. Either two sentences or a comma.

Comma restraint. A single well-placed comma is fine. Stacking three or four commas in one sentence is the AI-prose tell. Break the sentence into two.

Oxford comma optional, used when needed for clarity, not by default.

Single quotation marks for titles and emphasis ('first-time buyer', 'foreign income'), double for actual quotations.

### Headings

Headings are descriptive, not clever. The reader should know what the section is about without reading it.

H1 is the page title and should contain the target keyword naturally. "UK Mortgages for Expats" not "Expats abroad? Read this".

H2s segment the page into scannable chunks. Each H2 should answer or address something.

H3s subdivide H2s only where the depth justifies it.

### Paragraph length

Paragraphs are 1-4 sentences. Long blocks of text are visually unwelcoming and do not survive mobile reading.

### Lists

Use sparingly. A bulleted list is the right format for a genuinely list-shaped piece of information (eligibility criteria, document checklists, lender requirements). It is the wrong format for prose that has been chopped up.

Tables for any comparison of three or more entities across the same dimension. "Lender / Min deposit / Min income / Foreign currencies accepted" is a table, not three sentences.

## 5. Conversion voice and CTAs

CTAs are the most important copy on the site. They get more attention proportionally and any weakness is amplified.

### The standard primary CTA

The standard CTA across the site is:

> **Contact us here. A mortgage broker will usually respond immediately.**

This is reused on hub pages, situation pages, programmatic pages, and most articles without modification. The form sits directly below it on the page, so "here" is unambiguous.

The "respond immediately" line is a real differentiator and is non-negotiable. UK service in this market is typically slow, and the contrast is something prospects notice. The line is honest because the introducer team monitors enquiries continuously and a mortgage broker does in fact usually respond immediately during operating hours.

### Contextual variations (used selectively)

A handful of page types benefit from a contextual lead-in before the standard CTA. Use these only where the page genuinely calls for it. Default is the standard CTA above.

**Calculator pages (after the result):**

> Want help making sense of these numbers? Contact us here. A specialist mortgage broker will usually respond immediately.

**Country pages:**

> Living in [Country] and looking at a UK mortgage? Contact us here. A specialist mortgage broker who knows your situation will usually respond immediately.

### WhatsApp is a separate route

WhatsApp is provided through a floating pill button on every page (number `+44 7395 549408`). It is not woven into the inline CTAs. The button is always present, the inline CTA stays clean.

The floating WhatsApp button opens with a pre-filled message: "Hi, I have a UK mortgage enquiry."

### Sticky elements

A sticky right-hand CTA on hub and situation pages contains the form opener. The floating WhatsApp pill sits in the bottom right on desktop and integrates into the bottom CTA bar on mobile.

### CTA verbs

Strong verbs: "Contact", "Send", "Tell us", "Ask", "Message". Verbs to avoid: "Click", "Submit", "Learn more", "Find out".

The words "get" and "got" are off the table everywhere on the site, not just in CTAs. They sit too close to the pushy ad-copy register the brand avoids.

"Click here" is forbidden. Links describe their destination.

### Form field labels and microcopy

Form labels are direct: "Your name", "Email", "What can we help with?". Not "Please enter your full name as it appears on your passport".

Helper text under fields is rare and only used to prevent friction: "We'll only use this to reply to your enquiry."

Submit button: "Send enquiry", never "Submit".

Post-submission message:

> Thank you. Your enquiry has been passed to a specialist mortgage broker. You should hear from them very shortly. If anything is urgent, message us on WhatsApp at +44 7395 549408.

### Urgency without manipulation

We do not manufacture urgency ("Limited time only!"). We do communicate genuine urgency where it exists ("Mortgage offers typically need to be in place 30 days before exchange").

We do not run countdown timers, fake stock counters, or "12 people are looking at this now" patterns. They are insulting to a sophisticated audience and damage credibility.

## 6. Contact channels at launch

Two routes, both go to the same place.

**Web form.** Inline on every hub, situation, programmatic, and article page. Long-form version at `/enquiry`. Three to seven fields depending on context. Submitted enquiries are reviewed by the introducer team and forwarded to an FCA-authorised mortgage broker.

**WhatsApp.** Number `+44 7395 549408`. Floating button on every page. Monitored continuously during operating hours. First response is from the introducer team, then handover to a mortgage broker for the regulated conversation.

**No inbound phone calls.** The number above is for WhatsApp messaging only. Phone is not added at launch because it adds operational complexity for cross-time-zone audiences. May be revisited in Phase 5.

**No live chat.** A WhatsApp button performs the same function with lower operational load and no widget bloat.

**No public email address.** Form and WhatsApp only. An auto-reply email confirms receipt after form submission.

## 7. What we never do

A short list of anti-patterns. If a draft contains any of these, it goes back.

**Never confuse the persona model.** The site is "we" for editorial and platform mechanics. The mortgage broker is "a specialist" or "a mortgage broker" for advice, suitability, and response. Never blur the two.

**Never use phrases that signal AI prose.** "It's important to note that...", "Whether you're X or Y...", "In today's [adjective] world...", "Navigating the complex world of...", "When it comes to...", "Embark on", "Unlock", "Empower", "Game-changer", "Cutting-edge", "Revolutionary".

**Never describe ourselves with adjectives we have not earned.** "Trusted", "leading", "best", "expert", "premier" all read as ad copy unless backed by something concrete. Replace with the concrete thing: "Used by 5,000 enquirers in 2025" beats "trusted".

**Never reference a third party with marketing language.** "FCA-authorised specialist brokers" is fine. "Top-rated, award-winning brokers" is not. The site does not vouch beyond what is verifiable.

**Never moralise or finger-wag.** "You should always..." is the wrong register. Inform, do not lecture.

**Never use second-person commands without earning them.** Pushy CTAs read as ad copy. The brand earns the click with credible content, not by demanding it.

**Never use "etc." or "and more".** Either list the things or rewrite to remove the need for the catch-all. Vague endings undermine credibility.

**Never close a paragraph or page with a rhetorical question.** "So what does this mean for you?" is filler. Cut.

**Never use exclamation marks** (with very rare exceptions, perhaps one per page maximum on confirmation messages).

**Never reference other entities in the founder's commercial life.** This site stands alone.

## 8. Examples, good and bad

### Hub-page opening

**Bad (AI-style padding):**
> When it comes to securing a UK mortgage as an expat, the process can seem complex and overwhelming. With so many factors to consider, it's important to understand all of your options before embarking on this important journey.

**Good (the same idea, written properly):**
> If you live outside the UK and want a UK mortgage, your options are narrower than they would be for a UK resident, but they exist. About 15 lenders work routinely with expat applicants, and the right one for you depends on where you live, what currency you earn in, and what kind of property you are buying.

### Lender criteria description

**Bad (vague, generic):**
> HSBC offers expat mortgages with competitive rates and flexible criteria, making them a popular choice for many expat borrowers.

**Good (specific, useful):**
> HSBC Expat lends to UK nationals living abroad in approximately 30 supported countries. Minimum loan size £100,000. Foreign currencies accepted include USD, EUR, AED, SGD, HKD. They underwrite to a UK affordability standard, applying a 25% haircut to most foreign income.

### CTA paragraph

**Bad (pushy ad copy):**
> Don't miss out! Take the first step toward your UK mortgage today. Click here now to speak to one of our award-winning brokers.

**Bad (extractive register):**
> Send us your details. We will respond within the hour.

**Good:**
> Contact us here. A mortgage broker will usually respond immediately.

### Article opening

**Bad (waffle):**
> The world of expat mortgages can be a confusing one, with many borrowers finding themselves unsure of where to turn. In this comprehensive guide, we'll walk you through everything you need to know.

**Good (lead with answer):**
> Most British expats qualify for a UK mortgage. The deposit is higher than for a UK resident (typically 25-35% rather than 5-15%), the rate is slightly more expensive (usually 0.3-0.7% above standard products), and the lender pool is smaller (around 15 active lenders). Beyond that, the application is similar.

## 9. Words and phrases to use, and to avoid

### Use freely

Specialist, criteria, application, eligibility, lender, broker, deposit, repayment, fixed rate, variable rate, interest only, capital repayment, ICR, LTV, foreign income, residency, tax residency, UK national, non-resident, expat, situation, options, suitable, depends on, typically, generally, usually, around, approximately, often.

### Avoid

Best, top, leading, premier, award-winning, world-class, cutting-edge, revolutionary, game-changing, seamless, journey, navigate, embark, unlock, empower, dream home, peace of mind, hassle-free, streamlined, bespoke (overused), tailored (overused), competitive (vague), exclusive (almost always untrue), best-in-class, end-to-end, holistic, **get**, **got**.

### Use carefully

"Expert" is acceptable applied to the brokers we forward to (they are FCA-authorised specialists), not to the site itself. "Trusted" is acceptable only where it is backed by a Trustpilot rating or similar third-party signal that can be linked.

## 10. Tone variations by page type

Voice principles do not change. Density and tone shade slightly by page type.

**Home (T1):** Warmest. Short sentences. Direct value proposition. Quickly orients and routes. Reads in under 30 seconds.

**Hub pages (T2):** Most authoritative register. Comprehensive. Reads as a reference. Long enough to be the definitive source on the topic, structured enough to scan.

**Situation pages (T3):** Empathetic but factual. Acknowledges the specific situation in the opening paragraph, then moves into criteria and options.

**Programmatic pages (T4):** Driest register. Data-led. Country/currency/lender-specific facts. The voice is reference-book rather than article.

**Calculators (T5):** Minimal copy around the tool. Functional labels. Light explanation underneath. Strong post-calculation CTA.

**Articles (T6):** Most editorial register. Author voice can be slightly more present, observations welcome, opinions appropriate where useful. Still under the "we" persona for editorial activity, no first-person "I".

**Operational (T7):** Plainest. Privacy and terms read as legal documents (because they are). About reads as a clear statement of what the site does and does not.

## 11. Compliance considerations

This is a non-advisory site. Voice must support that position consistently.

**Never write or imply advice from the site.** "We can help you find the best mortgage" implies advice. "We pass your enquiry to a mortgage broker who will assess your circumstances and recommend a lender" does not.

**Never use words that imply suitability assessment from the site.** "Best for you", "right for your situation", "perfect mortgage" all imply judgment we are not authorised to make. "A mortgage broker can assess suitability" is the correct framing.

**Risk wording.** Standard regulatory wording ("Your home may be repossessed if you do not keep up repayments on your mortgage") appears where required, in plain text, not buried.

**Introducer disclosure.** The introducer position is stated clearly in the About page, in the footer, and on the Privacy Policy. Tone here is plain explanation, not legal mumbling.

These compliance points will be revisited and tightened by a compliance consultant before launch (parked under P001).
